Pathway for Career

What is the Pathway I've Chosen?

One post-secondary pathway that leads into Aerospace engineering is the U of T Engineering Science program (EngSci). EngSci covers the basics of engineering in the first two years of the program, and then allows students to specialize in a subfield of engineering. Hence, I will enter the EngSci program and later specialize in Astronautical Engineering. I chose the university pathway because it allows me to have strong theoretical knowledge of the principles of Aerospace engineering. Subjects such as aerodynamics, orbital mechanics, and materials science are crucial to the design of aircraft. Without this theoretical knowledge, my performance as an engineer would be severely limited. I chose specifically the EngSci program because it is one of the most rigorous engineering programs in Canada. The faculty that runs the program ranks first in all of Canada, making it the best education in Aerospace engineering I can get.

What are the Requirements for Admission?

  • English (ENG4U/EAE4U)
  • Advanced Functions (MHF4U)
  • Calculus & Vectors (MCV4U)
  • Chemistry (SCH4U)
  • Physics (SPH4U)
  • 95.1% average

Likes and Concerns About the Program

Three things I like about the program are its rigor, the opportunities it offers, and its unique structure. Three concerns I have are the workload of the program, the potential drawbacks to its structuring, and the lack of industry experience. First of all, UoFT’s Engineering Science Program (EngSci) is an incredibly rigorous program. According to the UoFT Faculty of Engineering website, it is ranked first in all of Canada and is also highly competitive. I like this rigor because it will help mold and refine me into the best engineer I can be. The rigor gives me a low tolerance for error and, hence, drastically improves my performance as an engineer. In addition, I also really like the opportunities that the program presents. EngSci provides me with the opportunity to do a Co-op placement in many engineering-related companies around the world through their PEY Co-op Program. This program gives me the chance to feel what it is like to work in an actual engineering corporation, building experience for future decisions and job applications. Another thing I like about the program is its unique structure. The EngSci program is organized in a “2+2” structure. The first two years are set aside to strengthen the foundations of engineering (e.g. Math), and the last two years are for your specialized major. I like this structure because it ensures a strong foundation, hence ensuring that I am prepared for my major. The fundamentals are crucial to get right, as a weak foundation will limit an engineer’s performance. Moving forward, my first concern is the workload of the program. Since the program is very rigorous and difficult, I may not have the capacity to handle this workload. I am concerned that I might drop out or lag behind due to the stress of the coursework. In addition, the potential drawback of the structure of EngSci is of concern. Though the 2+2 structure is incredibly beneficial, it may also cause its graduates to lag behind. This is because the program forces its students to wait 2 years until they can specialize, instead of allowing them to specialize earlier. This would make certain students fall behind compared to other students in traditional programs. This may cause me to fall behind compared to my peers in different programs. Finally, my last concern about EngSci is the lack of industry experience in the program. Though Engineering Science does have amazing opportunities through PEY, it is very little compared to other programs at other universities. This may present a massive disadvantage when job searching, as my work experience may be overshadowed by other candidates. In conclusion, the rigor, unique structure, and opportunities are some things I like about this program, while the workload, disadvantages of the structure, and lack of industry exposure are concerns I have about EngSci.
